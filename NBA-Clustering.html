Clustering of NBA Players

Yiding Hou | Fri 08 June 2018

Introduction¶

I wanted to test out clustering algorithmns on NBA player stats to try and see if they could differentiate between the five NBA player positions: Point Guard (PG), Shooting Guard (SG), Small Forward (SF), Power Forward (PF), and Center (C). More recently in the NBA there has been more fluidity in the positions: Giannis has been labeled as a PG/SF/PF at various times in the past couple years, and Draymond Green PF often plays C in the small ball lineup for the Golden State Warriors. I want to see how this affects the results of clustering.

I am using NBA data from the 2017-18 basketball season from basketball reference. I want to only look at starting players. Since there is no starter designation in my data, I am looking the top 150 players in terms of average minutes per game with a minimum of 20 games played.

Here is my data; it shows the season averages of players. The 'Fantasy_Points' columns is based on Draftkings fantasy scoring. I am standardizing the values in order for better clustering results since points has higher variability than assists and rebounds. If I were to leave the values unstandardized, then the clusters would simply split the player between high, medium and low scorers.

KMeans Clustering¶

I am using the KMeans algorithmn to cluster on points, assists, and rebounds as these are the most basic NBA stats. KMeans splits the space into n buckets and minimize the sum of square distances (SSD) between points in the bucket to the center of the bucket. An important decision is the number of clusters to use. A common method used is to graph the number of clusters vs SSD to see where there are dimishing returns to adding more clusters. According to the graph below, three clusters is the theoretical choice, however I am using five clusters to represent the positions.

from sklearn.cluster import KMeans

sum_squares = list()
for num in range(1,11):
    kmeans = KMeans(init='random', n_clusters = num)
    kmeans.fit(starters[["REB_std","AST_std", "PTS_std"]])
    ssd = kmeans.inertia_
    sum_squares.append(ssd)

Below is the results of KMeans clustering on standardized PTS, AST, and REB. Based on my discretion, I named these clusters point guards, all stars, shooting guards, forwards, and benchwamers. These clusterings aren't perfect; some notable misclassifications are Lou Williams, Dennis Schroder, and Blake Griffin (overrated) as all stars.

The main problem with this clustering is that it splits all stars and benchwarmers rather than the positions. One way of dealing with this is clustering on the proportion of fantasy output that PTS, AST, and REB account for. This standardizes across skill level.

This clustering achieves a pretty good split of players by position. The size of the markers in the plot are proportional to fantasy output. The hardest positions to distinguish is between SG and SF. This is pretty representative on the modern NBA where both positions share similar roles.

It is interesting that Nikola Jokic and Draymond Green, both PFs, are being clustered as PGs. Nikola Jokic is touted as the best passing big man in the game, and assists are a much bigger part of his fantasy output than other centers and power forwards, which explains why he is clustered with the point guards. Also, in the Golden State Warriors offense, Draymond is often passing to cutting shooters such as Stephen Curry, Klay Thompson, or Kevin Durant.

Here is a look at the positional makeup in each cluster. The x axis is the classification using the clustering algorithm and the stacked bars represent the actual position. As expected the clustering is a pretty good split of the positions.

Conclusion¶

KMeans clustering does a decent job at distinguishing between NBA player positions when clustering on the percentage of output that PTS, AST, and REB account for. Something I would want to explore next is applying this clustering to individual games and see what percentage that player spends in a cluster. This can be used to approach modeling players differently. For example if you wanted to predict fantasy output, modeling Nikola Jokic and Blake Griffin both as PFs is not accurate. Instead it might be better to model Nikola Jokic as 50% PG and 50% PF and Blake Griffin as 75% PF and 25% C.
