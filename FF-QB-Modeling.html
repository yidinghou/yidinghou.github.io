Learning to Rank QBs in Fantasy Football

Yiding Hou | Tue 20 November 2018

Ranking Fantasy Football QBs¶

This year, I started tracking fantasy football expert consensus rankings (ECR) from FantasyPros to see how accurate the experts are at ranking QBs. I chose the average of 13 experts based on their historical accuracy and reputation. I also want to see if I could create a better model for ranking QBs than the experts. I chose to rank QBs because QB streaming is a common strategy used in fantasy football.

QB Streaming¶

QB streaming is a fantasy football strategy where you choose not to draft a top QB and instead play roulette each week with QBs that can be picked up off the waivers. In my analysis of ECR, I am excluding the top 11 QBs each week. My reasoning is that if you have those QBs, you are almost definitely starting them and making the correct decision. When streaming, it is much more important to correctly rank QBs 12-32 as those are the ones available. Also, it is not useful to compare QB12 to Nathan Peterman (sorry bro), so I only want to compare players with similar fantasy output.

Ranking Accuracy¶

To evaluate ranking accuracy, I am using pariwise ranking. In this method, each QB is compared to all other QBs and you are trying to classify which one does better. For example if there are 4 QBs, then there are 6 comparisons made: 1v2, 1v3, 1v4, 2v3, 2v4, 3v4. If the highest ranked QB does the worst, but every other QB was in the correct relative ranking, the overall accuracy will be 50%. This may seem harsh because only one player was ranked incorrectly, but I think this is a more meaningful. If you choose to stream the highest ranked QB and he performs the worst, not only are you making an incorrect decision, but you also could have easily chose several better options. Appendix¶

Data Features¶

Here are the features I am using for modeling:

Salary (X[0]): DraftKings Salary
Value_Proj (X[1]): Calculated projection based on rolling average of value multiplied by DraftKings Salary
Mean_Proj (X[2]): Calculated projection based on rolling average of fantasy points
FC_Proj (X[3]): Fantasy Cruncher projections
Vegas_Points (X[4]): Implied vegas team points based on spread and over under
WR_Salary (X[5]): Average wide received DraftKings Salary for the respective QB

When creating the pairwise dataset, I am taking the difference between these features to pairwise compare QBs.

Decision Tree¶

My first pass at modeling is fitting a decision tree. This is a rule based flowchart model that creates predictions by splitting the data according to features. The model is fit on data from 2016-17 and is tested on 2018. I am limiting the depth of the decision tree to three layers to avoid overfitting. Below is a visualisation of the decision tree. This model has a 62.1% accuracy for 2018 vs 63.1% accuracy for ECR.

Random Forest¶

A random forest model is creates several random decision trees based on features, and uses the average prediction of those decision trees to make final predictions. Before fitting random forests to the data, we have to reduce correlation in our features. A random forest model randomly selects a subset of features to fit a decision tree on, so if there are correlated features, then that trend will be more likely to be selected which will result in the model giving too much important to that trend. Here is a look at feature correlations. Salary_Diff is highly correlate with all the other features so I am removing that. Value_Proj_Diff and Mean_Proj_Diff are both highly correlated with each other; I am removing Value_Diff using a similar reasoning as above.

Another step in fitting a random forest model is to tune parameters to prevent overfitting. I am tuning the split size; which is the minimum size after each split in the tree. The smaller the split size, the more litkely the model is overfitting. For example, if you keep creating rules and splitting the data until each observations is in its own group, then the model will achieve 100% accuracy. However this model has very little predictive power for futures observations.

K Fold Cross Validation¶

In order to select the minimum allowable split size, I am performing k fold cross validation and comparing the training accuracy vs the testing accuracy. K fold cross validation is a technique of splitting the data into K sets, training the model on K-1 of the sets and testing that model on the remaining. This process is then repeated K times. I want the split size to be as small as possible without having the training accuracy deviate too much from the testing accuracy. The graph below shows the difference between trainng and testing accuracy for various split sizes. It looks like a split size around 250 is a good selection.

Caveats¶

A major caveat in predicting fantasy football is that type of data available. If there were more pulicly available data on play calling, defensive schemes, route running, and CB vs WR matchups, more advanced and predictive features could be generated. As it stands now, the features I use are just projections from other sources such as DraftKings and Fantasy Cruncher. There simply isn't enough data at this point to outperform the experts who probably look at more advanced data or watched film.
