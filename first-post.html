Clustering of NBA Players

Yiding Hou | Fri 08 June 2018

Introduction¶

Basketball is played with 5 positons: Point Guard (PG), Shooting Guard (SG), Small Forward (SF), Power Forward (PF), and Center (C). More recently in the NBA there has been more fluidity in the positions. Giannis has been labeled as a PG/SF/PF. LeBron and Ben Simmons are often referred to as Point Forwards. I am exploring the use of clustering algorithmns to classify the 5 positons to try and find an objectve way of designating player positions.

I am using NBA data from the 2017-18 basketball season from basketball reference. I want to only look at starting players. Since there is no starter designation in my data, I am looking the top 150 players in terms of average minutes per game with a minimum of 20 games played.

Q = """
SELECT * FROM PLAYER_DATA P JOIN SALARY_DATA S 
ON P.Player = S.Player 
AND P.Month = S.Month 
AND P.Day = S.Day 
AND P.Year = S.Year"""
start = dt.datetime(month = 9, day = 1, year = 2017)

#nba_player_data is a pandas dataframe with game logs of all nba players 

player_agg = pd.concat([nba_player_data.groupby(["Player"])["Month"].count(),
                       nba_player_data.groupby(["Player"])["PTS","AST","REB","Minutes","Fantasy_Points"].mean()], 
                       axis=1)
player_agg.rename(columns={"Month":"Count"}, inplace=True)

starters = player_agg[player_agg["Count"]>20]
starters.sort_values("Minutes", inplace=True)
starters = starters.tail(150)

Here is my data; it shows the season averages of players. The 'Fantasy_Points' columns is based on Draftkings fantasy scoring. I am standardizing the values in order for better clustering results since points has higher variability than assists and rebounds. If I were to leave the values unstandardized, then the clusters would simply split the player between high, medium and low scorers.

from sklearn.preprocessing import MinMaxScaler

KMeans Clustering¶

I am using the KMeans algorithmn to cluster on points, assists, and rebounds as these are the most basic NBA stats. KMeans tries to split the space into n buckets and minimize the sum of square distances (SSD) between points in the bucket to the center of the bucket. An important decision is to decide the number of clusters to use. A common method used is to graph the number of clusters vs SSD to see where there are dimishing returns to adding more clusters.

from sklearn.cluster import KMeans
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

sum_squares = list()
for num in range(1,11):
    kmeans = KMeans(init='random', n_clusters = num)
    kmeans.fit(starters[["REB_std","AST_std", "PTS_std"]])
    ssd = kmeans.inertia_
    sum_squares.append(ssd)

plt.plot(range(1,11), sum_squares, 'bx-')
plt.xlabel('k')
plt.ylabel('Sum_of_squared_distances')
plt.show()

from sklearn.cluster import KMeans

kmeans_std = KMeans(init='random', n_clusters = 5, random_state=3425)
kmeans_std.fit(starters[["PTS_std","AST_std", "REB_std"]])
starters["Cluster"] = kmeans_std.labels_
starters["Cluster"].replace({0:0, 1:4, 2:2, 3:3, 4:1}, inplace=True)

Even though the theoretical optimal number of clusters is 3, I am using 5 clusters to attempt to represent the 5 positions.

I named these clusters point guards, all stars, shooting guards, forwards, and benchwamers to denote what I thought each cluster represented. These clusterings aren't perfect; some notable misclassifications are Lou Williams, Dennis Schroder, and Blake Griffin (overrated) as all stars.

In order to get rid of the undesirable all star/benchwarmer split, the data needs to be standardized across skill level. One way of achieve this is to normalize by fantasy points by calculating the percentage of fantasy points that points, assists, and rebounds account for. I am using Draftkings fantasy scoring rules.

starters["FP"] = starters["PTS"] + 1.5*starters["AST"] + 1.25*starters["REB"]
starters["REB%"] = 1.25*starters["REB"]/starters["FP"]
starters["AST%"] = 1.5*starters["AST"]/starters["FP"]
starters["PTS%"] = starters["PTS"]/starters["FP"]

kmeans_perc = KMeans(init='random', n_clusters = 5, random_state=1)
kmeans_perc.fit(starters[["PTS%","AST%", "REB%"]])
starters["Cluster"] = kmeans_perc.labels_

This clustering achieves a pretty good split of players by position. The size of the markers in the plot are proportional to fantasy output. It is hard to distinguish between shooting guards and small forwards. I think this is pretty representative on the modern NBA where "small ball" is all the rage and both positions share a lot of similar roles.

One interesting misclassification is Nikola Jokic and Al Horford, both centers, being classified as point guards. Nikola Jokic is touted as the best passing big man in the game, and assists are a much bigger part of his fantasy output than other centers and power forwards, which explains why he is clustered with the point guards.

Conclusion¶

Using KMeans clustering on the percentage that points, assists, and rebounds account for fantasy points is a pretty good way of determining position. One additonal step I want to explore is using this clustering on individual game logs to see what proportion of the time a player is classified as a certain position. For example, LeBron could be 60% SF and 40% PG. Another future step is to use other stats to cluster players. The theoretical optimal clustering using points, assists, and rebounds is 3 groups, indicating that these are not the best stats to split the 5 postions. Some other stats to try out are: three pointers made, field goal percentage, offensive rebounds, fouls. Stay tuned for a future update!
